Section 13: Phase Portraits and Stability

Our final topic will be nonlinear systems, like the Lotka-Volterra model for predator-prey relationships. Some of the methods we’ll use to understand and classify them apply to linear systems too, and so we’ll start there. First of all, we’ll need a few definitions of vector field objects.

Definition: sources,

In a vector field, a critical point is a point $(x, y)$ for which there is no motion. If the field around a critical point exclusively flows away from it, the point is called a source. If the surrounding field exclusively flows toward the point, it’s called a sink. If some flows toward the point and some flows away, it’s called a saddle point. Finally, if the field simply spirals around the point and none flows toward or away, it’s called a center.

In the field below, the top-right critical point is a source, the bottom-left is a sink, and the other two are saddle points.

Let’s look at the system $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{Ax}$. As we touched on in homework 8, the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{A}$ determine the general behavior of the system — let’s summarize the possibilities here.

If $\mathbf{A}$ is a $2 \times 2$ matrix with real entries and nonzero eigenvalues $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$, then there is a single critical point at the origin, and the system has the following characteristics:

1. If $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are real and positive, then the critical point is a source.

2. If $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are real and negative, then the critical point is a sink.

3. If $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are real and have opposite signs, then the critical point is a saddle point.

4. If $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are complex conjugates and the real part is positive, then the critical point is a center.

5. If $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are complex conjugates and the real part is negative, then the critical point is a sink about which the field spirals.

6. If $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are complex conjugates and the real part is zero, then the field simply spirals about the critical point.

These six possibilities are explored thoroughly in homework 8, but it’d be nice to avoid memorizing all of this, and hopefully to understand the meaning behind it all a bit more. The eigenvectors of the matrix determine the lines along which the field flows to or from the source, and the sign of the real part of the corresponding eigenvalues determines whether that flow is toward the source (negative) or away (positive). Imaginary parts of eigenvalues introduce curl into the system, turning the field into a spiral.

Drag the red point to change the eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The blue curves are sample solutions, and the ones marked in black are the two fundamental solutions — all together, we call this a phase portrait of the system.

Example: phase

Given the following phase portrait for $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{Ax}$, what can we determine about the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $\mathbf{A}$?

Since all of the solutions approach straight lines as $t \to \infty$ or $t \to -\infty$, there is no curl in this system — the eigenvalues and eigenvectors must be real. The eigenvectors are parallel to the straight-line solutions (being on a straight-line solution is what it means to be an eigenvector of $\mathbf{A}$), so the eigenvectors appear to be

$$\begin{align*}\mathbf{v_1} = \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right], \quad \mathbf{v_2} = \left[\begin{array}{c} -3 \\ 1 \end{array}\right].\end{align*}$$

What about the eigenvalues? The fundamental solution corresponding to $\mathbf{v_1}$ flows toward the origin, so its eigenvalue $\lambda_1$ must be negative. On the other hand, the fundamental solution corresponding to $\mathbf{v_2}$ flows away from the origin, so its eigenvalue $\lambda_2$ must be positive.

Autonomous Systems

Let’s take our first step into nonlinear systems. Since we were barely able to solve any nonlinear individual first-order DEs, we shouldn’t expect to be able to solve any nonlinear system. Instead, we’ll work with a particular kind of nonlinear system: one that doesn’t involve $t$ at all.

Definition: autonomous

A system of two DEs is autonomous if it is of the form

$$\begin{align*}x' &= f(x, y)\\[4px]y' &= g(x, y)\end{align*}$$

for continuous functions $f$ and $g$.

For a nonlinear system, we don’t have nearly as nice of a classification of critical points. Instead, we’ll just classify them as stable or unstable, depending on whether trajectories stay near the point or don’t. Stable solutions are effectively sinks and centers, and unstable ones are all the rest. Analyzing autonomous systems can be quite hard in general, but for particularly nice functions $f$ and $g$, it’s possible to solve explicitly — typically, we’ll hope to use a modified form of separation of variables.

Example: an

Solve the system

$$\begin{align*}x' &= y^2\\[4px]y' &= 1 - x^2.\end{align*}$$

Find and classify the critical points.

Since neither $x'$ or $y'$ depends on $t$, this system is autonomous. The chain rule says that $\frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{dy}{dx}\frac{dx}{dt}$, so solving for $\frac{dy}{dx}$ gives us

$$\begin{align*}\frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}\\[4px]&= \frac{y'}{x'}\\[4px]&= \frac{1 - x^2}{y^2}.\end{align*}$$

To solve for $y$, we can separate and integrate.

$$\begin{align*}y^2\,dy &= (1-x^2)\,dx\\[4px]\int y^2\,dy &= \int (1-x^2)\,dx\\[4px]\frac{y^3}{3} &= x - \frac{x^3}{3} + c\\[4px]x^3 + y^3 - 3x &= c.\end{align*}$$

Varying $c$, we can see all of the different trajectories a point can follow in the field.

To find the critical points, we just solve $x' = y' = 0$. That means $y^2 = 0$ and $1 - x^2 = 0$, so $y = 0$ and $x = \pm 1$. Classifying them is more complex, and for us, it frequently requires inspecting the vector field. Here, we see that both critical points are unstable, since trajectories near the points don’t fall into them.

Locally Linear Systems

Compared to linear systems, where there’s a single critical point at the origin and its behavior is well understood — it’s stable exactly when the real part of both eigenvalues are less than or equal to zero — determining the stability of a critical point in a nonlinear system can be tricky without a vector field. Thankfully, we can use an analogue of linear approximation to almost completely fix the problem.

Definition: locally

A system of DEs $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})$ is locally linear if there is a matrix $\mathbf{A}$ so that

$$\begin{align*}\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{Ax} + \mathbf{G}(\mathbf{x})\end{align*}$$

and

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{||\mathbf{x}|| \to 0} \frac{||\mathbf{G}(\mathbf{x})||}{||x||} = 0,\end{align*}$$

where $||\mathbf{x}||$ is the length of $\mathbf{x}$.

This definition is fine, but we’ll barely use it — for us, the most important facts about locally linear systems will be one result classifying their critical points and another giving an easy way to compute $\mathbf{A}$ and determine if it exists.

Theorem: creating

Consider the system

$$\begin{align*}x' &= f(x, y)\\[4px]y' &= g(x, y),\end{align*}$$

where $f$ and $g$ have continuous first- and second-order partial derivatives in both $x$ and $y$. If $(x_0, y_0)$ is a critical point, then the system is locally linear near $(x_0, y_0)$, with the approximating matrix $\mathbf{A}$ given by

$$\begin{align*}\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{J}(x_0, y_0) = \left[\begin{array}{cccc} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(x_0& y_0)& \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x_0& y_0) \\ \frac{\partial g}{\partial x}(x_0& y_0)& \frac{\partial g}{\partial y}(x_0& y_0) \end{array}\right],\end{align*}$$

called the Jacobian of $f$ and $g$. Moreover, as long as the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{J}(x_0, y_0)$ are not pure imaginary, the stability of $(x_0, y_0)$ in the original system is the same as the stability of the origin as a critical point in the system

$$\begin{align*}\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{J}(x_0, y_0)\mathbf{x}.\end{align*}$$

With the Jacobian in hand, we can approximate complex systems and determine the behavior of critical points without plotting a vector field.

Example: creating

A damped pendulum swings back and forth with angle $\theta$ from the vertical (so $\theta = 0$ corresponds to the pendulum pointing straight down). If $x = \theta$ and $y = \theta'$, then $x$ and $y$ satisfy a system of first-order DEs:

$$\begin{align*}x' &= y\\[4px]y' &= -\frac{1}{2}y - \sin(x).\end{align*}$$

There is a critical point at $\left(\pi, 0\right)$. Is it stable or unstable?

To get started, let’s find the Jacobian in general:

$$\begin{align*}\mathbf{J} &= \left[\begin{array}{cc} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}& \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \\ \frac{\partial g}{\partial x}& \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} \end{array}\right]\\[4px]&= \left[\begin{array}{cc} 0& 1 \\ -\cos(x)& -\frac{1}{2} \end{array}\right].\end{align*}$$

Plugging in $x = \pi$ and $y = 0$ gives us

$$\begin{align*}\mathbf{J}\left(0, \pi\right) &= \left[\begin{array}{cc} 0& 1 \\ 1& -\frac{1}{2} \end{array}\right].\end{align*}$$

Now near this critical point, our nonlinear system has the same behavior as the linear system

$$\begin{align*}\mathbf{x}' = \left[\begin{array}{cc} 0& 1 \\ 1& -\frac{1}{2} \end{array}\right]\mathbf{x}.\end{align*}$$

Solving for the eigenvalues gives us

$$\begin{align*}-\lambda\left(-\frac{1}{2} - \lambda\right) - 1 &= 0\\[4px]\frac{1}{2} \lambda + \lambda^2 - 1 &= 0\\[4px]\lambda &= \frac{-\frac{1}{2} \pm \sqrt{\frac{1}{4} + 4}}{2}\\[4px]\lambda &= -\frac{1}{4} \pm \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{17}{4}}\\[4px]\lambda &= -\frac{1}{4} \pm \frac{\sqrt{17}}{4}.\end{align*}$$

Since $\sqrt{17} > 1$, one of those eigenvalues is positive and the other one is negative. Therefore, the critical point in the linear system is a saddle point. Since that’s unstable, the critical point in the nonlinear system must be too.
