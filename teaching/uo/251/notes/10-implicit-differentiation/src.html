!header Section 10: Implicit Differentiation !nav-buttons !begin-text-block We typically define functions explicitly. All that means is that $y$ is written as a function of $x$, so that we have an equation of the form $y = f(x)$. The plot of this function is all of the $(x, y)$-pairs that satisfy the equation, and since $y$ is on its own, we can just plug in a bunch of $x$-values to $f$ and get the $y$-values directly. It’s possible to write down functions without forcing $y$ to be alone on one side, though. For example, the equation $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ defines a curve: its points are given by all the $x$ and $y$ with $x^2 + y^2 = 1$. Now it’s not possible to solve for $y$ explicitly this way, since we’d have to take a square root, which results in $y = \pm \sqrt{1 - x^2}$. Visually, this means the curve doesn’t pass the vertical line test, which means it’s not a function. !desmos circle We can still talk about slopes of tangent lines, though. Pictured above is a graph of $x^2 + y^2 = 1$, along with two different tangent lines that share the same $x$-value. When a curve is defined with the $x$ and $y$ intermixed and not in the form $y = f(x)$, we say the curve is defined implicitly, and the process of finding the slopes of tangent lines is called implicit differentiation. The process takes a second to get used to, because it typically involves heavy use of the Chain Rule in situations where it might not seem to apply at first glance. !end-text-block !begin-thm implicit differentiation To find the derivative of a function defined implicitly in terms of $x$ and $y$: 1. Take $\frac{d}{dx}$ of both sides. While derivatives of $x$ work normally, $y$ is a function of $x$, so it’s necessary to use the Chain Rule. We have $\frac{d}{dx}[y] = \frac{dy}{dx}$, so for example, $\frac{d}{dx}[\tan(y)] = \sec^2(y) \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}$. 2. Move all terms containing $\frac{dy}{dx}$ to one side of the equation and all the other terms to the other side. 3. Factor out $\frac{dy}{dx}$ and divide by whatever is left over. !end-thm !begin-example implicit differentiation The curve $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ has two points with $x = \frac{1}{2}$. Find the slopes of the tangent lines for each point. First, let’s find $\frac{dy}{dx}$. We do this by differentiating both sides $dx$. $$ \begin{align*} x^2 + y^2 &= 1\\ \frac{d}{dx}[x^2 + y^2] &= \frac{d}{dx}[1]\\ \frac{d}{dx}[x^2] + \frac{d}{dx}[y^2] &= 0\\ 2x + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} &= 0\\ 2y\frac{dy}{dx} &= -2x\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\frac{x}{y}. \end{align*} $$ Notice how the derivative is in terms of both $x$ and $y$. It’s not enough to specify $x$, because this isn’t necessarily a function, so there can be multiple points with the same $x$-value. For our purposes, we need to find the points with $x$-coordinate $\frac{1}{2}$, so we solve $\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 + y^2 = 1$. That gives $y^2 = \frac{3}{4}$, and so $y = \pm \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$. Therefore, the slope of the tangent line at the top point is $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1/2}{\sqrt{3}/2} = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}. $$ Similarly, the slope at the bottom point is $\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$. This looks about right given the previous picture. !end-example !begin-exercise implicit differentiation The graph of $\cos(\sin(y)) = \cos(x)$ looks like a sequence of braids. !desmos braids Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$. Looking at the graph, the tangent line seems poorly defined when $x$ is a multiple of $2\pi$ and $y$ is a multiple of $\pi$. Explain what’s going on using the equation. !end-exercise !begin-example implicit differentiation Find $\displaystyle \frac{dy}{dx}$ from the equation $x\sin(xy) = y$. !desmos sin-thing We have $$ \begin{align*} x\sin(xy) &= y\\ \frac{d}{dx}[x\sin(xy)] &= \frac{d}{dx}[y]\\ \frac{d}{dx}[x] \cdot \sin(xy) + x \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[\sin(xy)] &= \frac{dy}{dx}\\ \sin(xy) + x \cos(xy) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[xy] &= \frac{dy}{dx}\\ \sin(xy) + x \cos(xy) \left(\frac{d}{dx}[x] \cdot y + x \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[y]\right) &= \frac{dy}{dx}\\ \sin(xy) + x \cos(xy) \left(y + x \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}\right) &= \frac{dy}{dx}\\ \sin(xy) + xy \cos(xy) + x^2\cos(xy) \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{dy}{dx}\\ (x^2\cos(xy) - 1) \frac{dy}{dx} &= -\sin(xy) - xy \cos(xy)\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= \frac{-\sin(xy) - xy \cos(xy)}{x^2\cos(xy) - 1}. \end{align*} $$ !end-example !section !text s Logarithmic Differentiation
!begin-text-block Implicit differentiation unlocks one extra technique: sometimes, difficult functions to differentiate become much easier when we take a log on both sides. Specifically, functions involving strange exponents will turn into multiplication, so we can use the product rule. So: if you have a function of the form $y = (f(x))^{g(x)}$, start by rewriting it as $$ \ln(y) = \ln((f(x))^{g(x)}) = g(x)\ln(f(x)), $$ and then differentiate implicitly. !end-text-block !begin-example logarithmic differentiation Find $\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}[x^x]$. Since $x$ is in both the base and the exponent, we can’t use either the derivative rules for $x^b$ or $b^x$. Instead, we write $$ \ln(y) = \ln(x^x) = x\ln(x), $$ and then differentiate implicitly to get $$ \begin{align*} \frac{1}{y} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} &= \ln(x) + x \cdot \frac{1}{x}\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= y(\ln(x) + 1)\\ \frac{dy}{dx} &= x^x(\ln(x) + 1). \end{align*} $$ !end-example !begin-exercise logarithmic differentiation Find $\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}\left[\sin(x)^{(e^x)}\right]$. !end-exercise !begin-text-block At long last, we can handle the derivative of $x^n$, where $n$ is not just a rational number, but any real number. Thankfully, it’s exactly what we’d like it to be. !end-text-block !begin-thm the derivative of $x^n$ for any real $n$ Let $n$ be a real number. Then $\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}$. !end-thm !begin-proof With $y = x^n$, write $$ \ln(y) = \ln(x^n) = n\ln(x). $$ Then $$ \frac{1}{y} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{n}{x}, $$ so $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = y\frac{n}{x} = \frac{nx^n}{x} = nx^{n-1}. $$ !end-proof !nav-buttons !footer
