Section 2.9: L’Hôpital’s Rule

After building a wide-ranging understanding of derivatives, let’s take a brief detour to return to the world of limits; some of them that were difficult to calculate before have very easy solutions with a single rule in terms of derivatives.

To briefly review, most common functions are continuous where they are defined, meaning $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ is very often just $f(a)$. The main times when this is not the case are the following:

1. If $f$ is a piecewise-defined function that changes definition at $a$, then we need to deal with both the one-sided limits, and the two-sided limit doesn’t exist unless both of those exist and are equal.

2. Evaluating $f(a)$ results in something of the form $\frac{c}{0}$ for a nonzero number $c$, in which case $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ is typically $\infty$ or $-\infty$.

3. The formula for $f$ is in some kind of form like $\frac{0}{0}$ at $a$, which is a lot more ambiguous. Sometimes, the limit will be $\pm \infty$, but other times, it will just be a finite number. We can often find that number by simplifying the expression somehow, but that simplification is often annoying or difficult or both, and any improvement to be had on this would be great. It’s this third case that we’ll be exploring in this section, and the first thing to do is to give names to the kind of forms like $\frac{0}{0}$.

Definition: indeterminate form

A limit is in indeterminate form if it results in any of the following:

1. $\displaystyle \frac{0}{0}$ or $\displaystyle \frac{\infty}{\infty}$.

2. $\displaystyle 0 \cdot \infty$.

3. $\displaystyle \infty - \infty$.

4. $\displaystyle 1^\infty$, $\displaystyle \infty^0$, or $\displaystyle 0^0$.

For example, $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x - 1}{x^2 - 3x + 2}$ is in indeterminate form, since we can’t plug in $x = 1$ without getting $\frac{0}{0}$. We could use some of the methods we learned in chapter 1 to rewrite it as

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x - 1}{x^2 - 3x + 2} &= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x - 1}{(x - 1)(x - 2)}\\[4px]&= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{1}{x - 2}\\[4px]&= -1.\end{align*}$$

As a reminder, we can perform that cancellation since even though the two functions aren’t equal, their limits are. The function $\frac{1}{x - 2}$ is equal to $\frac{x - 1}{x^2 - 3x + 2}$ everywhere except for at $x = 1$, and changing only one value of a function doesn’t change any of its limits.

Unfortunately, this method of factoring and canceling runs dry before too long. The limit

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{-\sin(\pi x)}{\ln(x)}\end{align*}$$

still has the form $\frac{0}{0}$, for example, but we’re unlikely to find a way to simplify it. Instead, let’s write down a much generalizable result.

Theorem: L’Hôpital’s Rule

Suppose $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ is of the form $\displaystyle \frac{0}{0}$ or $\displaystyle \frac{\infty}{\infty}$. Then

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}\end{align*}$$

if the second limit exists.

In other words, if we get $\frac{0}{0}$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ out of a limit, we can differentiate the top and bottom and try again. Two very important things to note: first of all, we can only do this if the limit is of one of those two forms — while it’d be nice to be able to differentiate the top and bottom of any limit, it only works for these. Secondly, we’re not using the Quotient Rule here: the top and bottom of the fraction get differentiated separately.

Example: L’Hôpital’s Rule

Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{-\sin(\pi x)}{\ln(x)}$.

We’ve already seen that this has the form $\frac{0}{0}$, so we can apply L’Hôpital’s Rule:

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{-\sin(\pi x)}{\ln(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\left[ -\sin(\pi x) \right]}{\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\left[ \ln(x) \right]}\\[4px]&= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{-\cos(\pi x)\cdot \pi}{1/x}\\[4px]&= \lim_{x \to 1} \left( -\pi x \cos(\pi x) \right)\\[4px]&= -\pi \cos(\pi)\\[4px]&= \pi.\end{align*}$$

Much easier than this process used to be!

To say just a bit about why this process works (at least in the $\frac{0}{0}$ case), let’s look at those two functions near $x = 1$.

Both functions are approaching zero, but since they’re differentiable, they’re well approximated by tangent lines. That is, near $x = 1$, $-\sin(\pi x) \approx \pi (x - 1)$ and $\ln(x) \approx (x - 1)$. Their ratio is then approximately $\frac{\pi (x - 1)}{x - 1} = \pi$, but that approximation notably only works because there is no constant term from a nonzero $y$-value in the tangent line formula — that’s why L’Hôpital’s Rule doesn’t apply in other forms.

What about the other indeterminate forms? Unfortunately, L’Hôpital’s Rule doesn’t apply to them directly; instead, we’ll need to rewrite them into either the form $\frac{0}{0}$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ or to the other indeterminate forms. Let’s start with the form $0 \cdot \infty$ and work our way down.

If $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) \cdot g(x) = 0 \cdot \infty$, since $f(x) \to 0$ and $g(x) \to \infty$, then we have two options. First, since $\frac{1}{g(x)} \to 0$, we can rewrite the limit as

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{1/g(x)},\end{align*}$$

which will then be in the form $\frac{0}{0}$. Alternatively, since $\frac{1}{f(x)} \to \infty$ or $-\infty$, we can rewrite the original limit as

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to a} \frac{g(x)}{1/f(x)},\end{align*}$$

which is in the form $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$. Either way, L’Hôpital’s Rule will apply.

Example: L’Hôpital’s Rule in the case $0 \cdot \infty$

Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to 2^+} (2x - 4)\ln(2x - 4)$.

We have $2x - 4 \to 0$ and $\ln(2x - 4) \to -\infty$, so this limit is of the form $0 \cdot \infty$. We can either move $2x - 4$ or $\ln(2x - 4)$ into the denominator — let’s try the natural log and see how it goes.

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to 2^+} (2x - 4)\ln(2x - 4) = \frac{2x - 4}{\frac{1}{\ln(2x-4)}}.\end{align*}$$

We need to differentiate the top and bottom here. The derivative of $2x - 4$ is just $2$, but $\frac{1}{\ln(2x-4)}$ is a Quotient Rule problem (or a Chain Rule one, depending on how you approach it). We have

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x} \left[ \frac{1}{\ln(2x-4)} \right] = \frac{-\frac{2}{2x - 4}}{\ln^2(2x - 4)},\end{align*}$$

which is looking significantly more complicated. We’ll probably have to run L’Hôpital’s Rule one more time at the very least, and there’s no guarantee it will get simpler. Instead, let’s go back and try the other route.

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to 2^+} (2x - 4)\ln(2x - 4) = \frac{\ln(2x-4)}{\frac{1}{2x - 4}}.\end{align*}$$

Now this is much easier to deal with. We have

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\left[\ln(2x - 4)\right] = \frac{2}{2x - 4}\end{align*}$$

and

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}x}\left[\frac{1}{2x - 4}\right] = \frac{-2}{(2x - 4)^2}.\end{align*}$$

Therefore, the limit becomes

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to 2^+} (2x - 4)\ln(2x - 4) &= \lim_{x \to 2^+} \frac{\frac{2}{2x - 4}}{\frac{-2}{(2x - 4)^2}}\\[4px]&= \lim_{x \to 2^+} \frac{2(2x - 4)^2}{-2(2x - 4)}\\[4px]&= \lim_{x \to 2^+} -(2x - 4)\\[4px]&= -(2\cdot 0 - 4)\\[4px]&= 0.\end{align*}$$

We handle the $\infty - \infty$ case similarly: we first rewrite both terms to be fractions, then combine them with common denominators in order to apply L’Hôpital’s Rule.

The final type of indeterminate form involves exponents. To handle these, we follow a process reminiscent of logarithmic differentiation: for $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)^{g(x)}$, we set $y = f(x)^{g(x)}$, take the natural log of both sides to get $\ln(y) = g(x) \ln(f(x))$, and then evaluate the limit on the right, possibly with L’Hôpital’s Rule. Since $\ln$ is continuous, $\lim_{x \to a} \ln(y) = \ln(\lim_{x \to a} y)$, so once we’ve found the limit on the right side, all we need to do is raise $e$ to that power to find the original limit we were looking for.

Example: L’Hôpital’s Rule in the case $1^\infty$

Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} \left( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right)^x$.

Since this is of the form $1^\infty$, we can write it as

$$\begin{align*}y &= \left( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right)^x\\[4px]\ln(y) &= \ln\left( \left( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right)^x \right)\\[4px]\ln(y) &= x \ln \left( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right)\\[4px]\lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \left( x \ln \left( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right) \right)\\[4px]\lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln \left( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right)}{1/x},\end{align*}$$

since that right-hand limit was in the form $0 \cdot \infty$. Now

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln \left( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right)}{1/x}\\[4px]\lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{1}{1 + 1/x}\left( -\frac{1}{x^2} \right) }{-\frac{1}{x^2}}\\[4px]\lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{1 + \frac{1}{x}}\\[4px]\lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) &= 1.\end{align*}$$

Since $\ln$ is a continuous function, we can rewrite the left side by moving the limit inside the $\ln$:

$$\begin{align*}\ln\left( \lim_{x \to \infty} y \right) &= 1.\\[4px]\lim_{x \to \infty} y &= e^1\\[4px]\lim_{x \to \infty} \left( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right)^x &= e.\end{align*}$$

Example: L’Hôpital’s Rule in the case $1^\infty$

Evaluate $\displaystyle \lim_{x \to \infty} (1 + e^{-x})^{x}$.

Since $e^{-x} \to 0$ and $x \to \infty$, this thing is of the form $1^\infty$. So let’s set $y = (1 + e^{-x})^{x}$ and take a natural log to get $\ln(y) = x\ln(1 + e^{-x})$. Now since $\ln(1) = 0$ and $\ln$ is continuous, $\ln(1 + e^{-x}) \to 0$, so the whole limit is now of the form $\infty \cdot 0$. We’ll tackle this just like we did before. We learned our lesson about putting the $\ln$ in the denominator, so let’s try the $x$ first this time. We get

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to \infty} x\ln(1 + e^{-x}) = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln(1 + e^{-x})}{1/x}.\end{align*}$$

Since this is of the form $\frac{0}{0}$, we can differentiate top and bottom to get

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to \infty} x\ln(1 + e^{-x}) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{-1}{1 + e^{-x}} \cdot e^{-x}}{-\frac{1}{x^2}}\\[4px]&= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2e^{-x}}{1 + e^{-x}}.\end{align*}$$

Unfortunately, we’re not quite done yet. While the denominator limits to $1$, the numerator is another case of $0 \cdot \infty$, since $x^2 \to \infty$ and $e^{-x} \to 0$. The easiest way to rewrite this as something that L’Hôpital’s Rule can handle is to move the $e^{-x}$ to the denominator to make it have a positive exponent:

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to \infty} x^2e^{-x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2}{e^x}.\end{align*}$$

Now this is of the form $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$, so we can differentiate top and bottom as usual.

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2}{e^x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2x}{e^x}.\end{align*}$$

Still $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$, so let’s use L’Hôpital’s Rule again.

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2}{e^x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2}{e^x}.\end{align*}$$

At long last, this is just $0$, since $e^x \to \infty$. Therefore, we have

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^2e^{-x}}{1 + e^{-x}} = \frac{0}{1} = 0,\end{align*}$$

and so

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) = \lim_{x \to \infty} x\ln(1 + e^{-x}) = 0.\end{align*}$$

As mentioned, we can move the limit on the left inside the $\ln$ and then exponentiate both sides to finally get that

$$\begin{align*}\lim_{x \to \infty} (1 + e^{-x})^{x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} y = e^0 = 1.\end{align*}$$
