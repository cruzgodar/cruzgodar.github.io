Section 6.5: Surface Integrals

With our plan in place from the previous section, let’s begin developing the tools we need to generalize Green’s Theorem, one at a time. The first goal we identified was to be able to integrate over a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$, rather than just a region in $\mathbb{R}^2$, and we’ll develop a technique to do just that in this section. We need to get our bearings with what a surface even looks like algebraically, though, or we won’t have a hope of knowing what it means to integrate over one.

Back in section 5.3, we discussed vector-valued functions. While functions that send one variable to two or more are interesting in their own right, we’ve primarily thought of them as curve parameterizations since then. In effect, a function $\vec{r}(t) = \left< x(t), y(t), z(t) \right>$ is a lens through which to understand its graph, a complicated 1-dimensional path in $\mathbb{R}^3$, as the operation of a function on an orderly set of inputs in $\mathbb{R}$ (often an interval $[a, b]$). Since we already have a well-established theory of functions, we can understand nearly everything about the curve in terms of $\vec{r}$.

There couldn’t be a better source of inspiration for building up a theory of surfaces. Rather than only a single input variable to a function $\vec{r}$, though, we’ll need two, since a surface is fundamentally a 2-dimensional object. Rather frustratingly, the standard choices are $u$ and $v$: distinguishable well enough on a chalkboard, but less so when typed. To help tell them apart, I’ll write $v$ as $\mathrm{v}$ throughout the remainder of these notes when it’s being used as a parameter along with $u$.

Definition: parametric surface

A parametric surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is a function $\vec{r} : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$; that is,

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v}) = \left< x(u, \mathrm{v}),\ y(u, \mathrm{v}),\ z(u, \mathrm{v}) \right>.\end{align*}$$

Most of our understanding of parametric surfaces will come from analogies to functions of two variables and parametric curves (which match the codomain and domain, respectively). To get a sense of how these two reference points interact, let’s think about derivatives. A vector-valued function $\vec{s}(t) = \left< a(t), b(t), c(t) \right>$ only had one input variable, and its derivative was

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} \left[ \vec{s}(t) \right] = \left< a'(t), b'(t), c'(t) \right>.\end{align*}$$

In contrast, a function $\vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v})$ has two inputs, and therefore two partial derivatives, each of which is a vector-valued function:

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\partial }{\partial u} \left[ \vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v}) \right] &= \vec{r}_u(u, \mathrm{v}) = \left< x_u(u, \mathrm{v}),\ y_u(u, \mathrm{v}),\ z_u(u, \mathrm{v}) \right>\\[4px]\frac{\partial }{\partial \mathrm{v}} \left[ \vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v}) \right] &= \vec{r}_\mathrm{v}(u, \mathrm{v}) = \left< x_\mathrm{v}(u, \mathrm{v}),\ y_\mathrm{v}(u, \mathrm{v}),\ z_\mathrm{v}(u, \mathrm{v}) \right>.\end{align*}$$

We’ll have more to say about those partial derivatives, but let’s turn to examples.
