Section 6.2: Path Integrals

We began discussing directional derivatives in part 5 out of a desire to understand derivatives more completely — partial derivatives were very literally a partial extension of the notion of a derivative to functions of multiple variables, and while directional derivatives didn’t immediately seem like the solution that would fill in the gaps, deriving a formula for them led us to the gradient, which ended up being a very important part of our journey.

We’ll see much of that story play out with integrals. Returning to the world of double integrals for a moment, the inner integral in

$$\begin{align*}\int_a^b\!\!\!\int_c^d f(x, y)\,\mathrm{d} x\,\mathrm{d} y\end{align*}$$

was able to find the area under the graph of a cross-section of $z = f(x, y)$; specifically, the cross-section with $y$ held fixed and $x$ ranging from $c$ to $d$. But what if we wanted to find a different area, like that of a cross-section in a different direction?

Having seen so many of the storylines of calculus at this point, we can reason through how this one might work: just like with directional derivatives, the fact that both $x$ and $y$ are changing at once as we move along this curve means we can’t get away with directly using single-variable calculus techniques like we could with partial derivatives or multiple integrals. Instead, we’ll need to parameterize the curve in the $xy$-plane along which this travels in terms of a single parameter $t$ (like we did with directional derivatives), and then likely use a Riemann sum to slice up the curve into small changes in $t$ (like we did with arc length).

But hold on a minute! That blueprint that we just laid out would work for a lot more than just lines. We could parameterize any path in the $xy$-plane and then integrate over it! It’s this idea that will really take off; let’s look at one path in particular and then go find a formula.

I highly recommend looking at this graph from multiple angles, then hiding the gray surface in the expressions list! The total area we’re measuring is complicated, but expressing it in terms of a Riemann sum is absolutely within reach.

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be a function, and let $\vec{r}(t)$ be a parameterized path in the $xy$-plane from $t = a$ to $t = b$. We cut the domain $[a, b]$ for $\vec{r}$ into $n$ subintervals that each cover $\Delta t$, and then we want to approximate the sum by rectangles.

This is quite the Riemann sum to take, but we’ve already seen all the tools we’ll need to understand it. For a particular rectangle centered at $t = t_0$, its height is given by the value of $f(x, y)$ at that point, and $x$ and $y$ are given by $\vec{r}(t_0)$; the height is therefore $f(\vec{r}(t_0))$. The base of the rectangle is slightly harder: it seems like it ought to be $\Delta t$, but that can’t quite be the whole story, since $\vec{r}(t)$ could cover any amount of distance as $t$ changes by $\Delta t$. To handle this carefully, we’ll need to approximate $\vec{r}(t)$ by line segments, and we’ve done this before — when we derived the integral for arc length! A line segment approximating $\vec{r}(t)$ near $t = t_0$ is given by $\vec{l}(t) = \vec{r}\left( t_0 \right) + \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t_0)t$; we’re interested in evaluating this for an interval of $t$-values whose width is $\Delta t$, so the total length of the line is $\left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t_0) \right| \right| \Delta t$. That becomes our rectangle’s base, and so its area is

$$\begin{align*}f(\vec{r}(t_0))\left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t_0) \right| \right| \Delta t.\end{align*}$$

In the limit, a sum of these areas becomes an integral, which we’ll want to give its own name.

Definition: path integral

Let $z = f(x, y)$ be a function, and let $C$ be a smooth curve in the $xy$-plane — that is, there is a parameterization by $\vec{r}(t)$ from $t = a$ to $t = b$ that is differentiable, where $\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)$ is continuous and never zero. The path integral of $f$ along $C$ is

$$\begin{align*}\int_C f(x, y)\,\mathrm{d} s = \int_a^b f(\vec{r}(t))\left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right|\,\mathrm{d} t.\end{align*}$$

The $s$ in the abstract integral on the left is a stand-in for a function that measures arc length along the curve; that is, $\mathrm{d} s = \left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right|\,\mathrm{d} t$. In practice, we nearly always need to parameterize the curve to take a path integral, and so we tend to engage with the version on the right as if it is the definition of a path integral, rather than the Riemann sum version.

Path integrals are often called line integrals in much of the literature on calculus. That’s a pretty unfortunate name, since we’re often integrating along paths that are very much nonlinear! We’ll stick with the slightly less common but more descriptive name path integral in these notes.

We can also take path integrals of functions of three or more variables, just without as nice of a visual to accompany them, and we’ll see the details of that in the next example. Before that, though, let’s formalize a nice property of path integrals that we can deduce from their geometry: we can chop up a curve into smaller portions in order to integrate along it more easily.

Proposition: the additivity of path integrals

Let $C_1$ and $C_2$ be two smooth curves in $\mathbb{R}^2$, such that $C_1$ ends where $C_2$ begins. If $C$ is the curve that combines them, following $C_1$ and then $C_2$, then for any continuous function $f(x, y)$,

$$\begin{align*}\int_C f(x, y)\,\mathrm{d} s = \int_{C_1} f(x, y)\,\mathrm{d} s + \int_{C_2} f(x, y)\,\mathrm{d} s.\end{align*}$$

Strictly speaking, this result is a definition of the integral $\int_C f(x, y)\,\mathrm{d} s$, since $C$ may not be smooth at the joining point of $C_1$ and $C_2$. In practice, though, this result means we can integrate functions along curves comprised of smooth parts (called piecewise smooth) by splitting them up into path integrals along each part. To avoid constantly referring to the term piecewise-smooth, we’ll define a path to be a piecewise-smooth curve.

Here, $C_1$ is the line from $(0, 0)$ to $(0, -2)$, and $C_2$ is the three-quarters circle of radius $2$ that extends clockwise from $(0, -2)$.

Example: a path integral in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Let $C$ be the path from $(1, 1, 0)$ to $(0, 1, 1)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ given by a quarter circle parallel to the $xz$-plane, and let $D$ be the path from $(1, 1, 0)$ to $(0, 1, 1)$ given by two line segments that meet at $(0, 1, 0)$, as shown.

Find $\int_C \left( x + y + z \right)\,\mathrm{d} s$ and $\int_D \left( x + y + z \right)\,\mathrm{d} s$.

We’ll begin by parameterizing both paths. $C$ is given by a circle, so we’d expect a cosine and sine to be present in there somewhere. Since we start from a point with $x = 1$, we can use cosine for the $x$-coordinate and sine for the $z$-coordinate, resulting in

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(t) = \left< \cos(t), 1, \sin(t) \right>\end{align*}$$

for $0 \leq t \leq \frac{\pi}{2}$. Then

$$\begin{align*}\left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)' \right| \right| &= \left| \left| \left< \cos(t), 1, \sin(t) \right> \right| \right|\\[4px]&= \sqrt{\cos^2(t) + 0 + \sin^2(t)}\\[4px]&= 1,\end{align*}$$

so our path integral becomes

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \left( x + y + z \right)\,\mathrm{d} s &= \int_0^{\pi / 2} \left( \cos(t) + 1 + \sin(t) \right) \cdot 1\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \left. \left[ \sin(t) + t - \cos(t) \right] \right|_0^{\pi / 2}\\[4px]&= 2 + \frac{\pi}{2}.\end{align*}$$

To integrate along $D$, we have to split it up with the previous proposition, since there would be a sharp corner in the graph at $(0, 1, 0)$ otherwise. Let’s call the two line segments $C_1$ and $C_2$ and parameterize them as

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r_1}(t) &= \left< 1, 1, 0 \right> + \left< -1, 0, 0 \right>t = \left< 1 - t, 1, 0 \right>\\[4px]\vec{r_2}(t) &= \left< 0, 1, 0 \right> + \left< 0, 0, 1 \right>t = \left< 0, 1, t \right>,\end{align*}$$

where $0 \leq t \leq 1$ for both. Then

$$\begin{align*}\left| \left| \vec{r_1}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right| &= \left| \left| \left< -1, 0, 0 \right> \right| \right| = 1\\[4px]\left| \left| \vec{r_2}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right| &= \left| \left| \left< 0, 0, 1 \right> \right| \right| = 1,\end{align*}$$

and so our integral is

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \left( x + y + z \right)\,\mathrm{d} s &= \int_{C_1} \left( x + y + z \right)\,\mathrm{d} s + \int_{C_2} \left( x + y + z \right)\,\mathrm{d} s\\[4px]&= \int_0^1 \left( (1 - t) + 1 + 0 \right) \cdot 1\,\mathrm{d} t + \int_0^1 \left( 0 + 1 + t \right) \cdot 1\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \left. \left[ 2t - \frac{t^2}{2} \right] \right|_0^1 + \left. \left[ t + \frac{t^2}{2} \right] \right|_0^1\\[4px]&= 2 - \frac{1}{2} + 1 + \frac{1}{2}\\[4px]&= 3.\end{align*}$$

But wait — that’s a different answer than we got by integrating along the circle! We’re running into a nuance of path integrals that we haven’t had to deal with in integration before; let’s take a closer look.

This example showed that path integrals are, generally speaking, path-dependent: integrating the same function along two paths, even if they have the same endpoints, can have different results. That’s bizarre from the perspective of single-variable integrals: the entire statement of the fundamental theorem of calculus is that the integral of a continuous function $f(x)$ on an interval $[a, b]$ depends only on the behavior of its antiderivative at the boundary points $a$ and $b$, not on how we got there. But then again, there was only one way to get there: in a straight line along the $x$-axis from $x = a$ to $x = b$. Comparing against the other type of integrals we’ve explored — double integrals — those always had the path picked for us as well, since both the inner and outer integrals were really just single-variable integrals out of their natural habitat.

We’re going to have a lot more to say about path-dependence later in this section, but for right now, I want to provide a little bit of intuition that path integrals can’t be path-independent in general. If we integrate the constant function $1$, then the path integral $\int_C 1\,\mathrm{d} s$ is equal to the arc length of $C$ for any path $C$; different paths connecting two given points will typically have different lengths, like in the previous example, and so those path integrals will necessarily be different.

Path Integrals Through Vector Fields

Single-variable integrals apply to much, much more than just finding areas under curves: whenever we want to add up a quantity that changes over time, distance, or with respect to any other variable, they’re the first tool we reach for. In the same way, if we’re adding up a changing quantity over the course of a path in 2D or 3D space, path integrals will be our starting point. For example, the integral of a density function along a thin wire gives the total mass of the wire.

In applications like this, there’s a quantity intrinsic to the object (like density) that’s changing along its length, but we can also explore a different type of changing quantity: one external to the path. That might seem like a just a slight twist on what we’ve already seen, but when we start pulling on this thread, it’ll prove to be a larger part of the whole picture than it first appeared.

Let’s say two river currents meet each other head-on, making the current flow out to either side orthogonally, and we decide to go for a swim in the middle (probably a profoundly bad idea with the potential for rip currents). At position $(x, y)$ in the river, the force of the current is $\vec{F}(x, y) = \left< -y, -x + y \right>$ (in Newtons, although we can mostly ignore units for this example). If we swim from $\left( 1, -\sqrt{3} \right)$ to $\left( 1, -\sqrt{3} \right)$ in a straight line, how much does the current help or hinder our forward progress?

The path we’re traveling along is the straight line from the blue point to the red point, and it doesn’t look like the most pleasant swim — we start off with the current strongly against us, and even when that lets up toward the end, there’s some side-to-side force that might make things difficult. The word forward in the question is doing a lot of work, though: since we’re always moving vertically in this picture, we’re being asked only how much the current impedes our vertical motion, and so we can ignore any horizontal component of the field. With that in mind, we’ll parameterize the straight line, as is becoming typical, and integrate the function given by the $y$-component of the field $\vec{F}$ over that path.

Let’s parameterize the line $L$ by

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(t) &= \left< 1, -\sqrt{3} \right> + \left< 0, 2\sqrt{3} \right>t\\[4px]&= \left< 1, -\sqrt{3} + 2\sqrt{3}t \right>, \quad 0 \leq t \leq 1\\[4px]~\\[4px]\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) &= \left< 0, 2\sqrt{3} \right>\\[4px]\left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right| &= 2\sqrt{3}.\end{align*}$$

Then our integral is

$$\begin{align*}\int_L (-x + y)\,\mathrm{d} s &= \int_0^1 \left( -1 -\sqrt{3} + 2\sqrt{3}t \right) \cdot 2\sqrt{3}\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \left. \left[ -2\sqrt{3}t - 6t + 6t^2 \right] \right|_0^1\\[4px]&= -2\sqrt{3}.\end{align*}$$

It’s reasonable that the result is negative — since the current was working against us nearly the entire time, that $y$-component was negative for most of the integral. On the graph, this is indicated by the color of the path: purple where the current is hindering our swimming, green where it’s helping, and white where it’s orthogonal and we’re considering it to have no effect.

This example was good to ease ourselves into the notion of integrating through a vector field, but things become a little more complicated when we don’t take so simple of a path. Let’s now consider swimming along the other path in the picture: the circular arc $C$. How can we compute the amount the current is helping us — that is, the amount it is flowing in our direction — at a given point along the path? Well, it matters what “our direction” is, and that’s a changing quantity. The direction in which we’re traveling in while on a path $\vec{r}(t)$ is given by $\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)$, and so we want to find the component of $\vec{F}(x, y)$, where $x$ and $y$ are given by $\vec{r}(t)$, in the direction of $\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)$. That’s a projection, and so we could compute

$$\begin{align*}\operatorname{proj}_{\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)} \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) = \frac{\vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \bullet \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)}{\left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right|^2}\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t).\end{align*}$$

However, let’s take a moment to simplify things instead of diving headfirst into that mess of a computation. First of all, projections are greatly simplified when the vector on which we’re projecting is normalized (i.e a unit vector), so we would do well to normalize $\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)$ before projecting. Moreover, we already have a name for the normalized derivative $\frac{\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)}{\left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right|}$: it’s the unit tangent vector $\vec{T}(t)$. Since it has magnitude $1$, the projection then becomes

$$\begin{align*}\operatorname{proj}_{\vec{T}(t)} \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) &= \frac{\vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \bullet \vec{T}(t)}{\left| \left| \vec{T}(t) \right| \right|^2} \vec{T}(t)\\[4px]&= \left( \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \bullet \vec{T}(t) \right) \vec{T}(t).\end{align*}$$

Now we’re interested not in this entire vector, but specifically its (signed) magnitude, also called the scalar projection: the length of the component of $\vec{F}(\vec{r}(t))$ that points in the direction of $\vec{T}(t)$, with a minus sign if it points in the opposite direction. Since $\vec{T}(t)$ is a unit vector, that’s conveniently just $\vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \bullet \vec{T}(t)$, and so that’s the quantity we’ll integrate along the path. To write it as a path integral pre-parameterization, we can suppress the inputs to $\vec{F}$ and $\vec{T}$ and just write

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \vec{F} \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s.\end{align*}$$

In practice, though, we don’t usually need to compute the unit tangent vector itself. That’s because the $\mathrm{d} s$ expands to $\left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right|\,\mathrm{d} t$, which cancels with $\vec{T}(t)$: if the path $C$ is parameterized by $\vec{r}(t)$ for $a \leq t \leq b$, then

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \vec{F} \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s &= \int_a^b \left( \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \bullet \vec{T}(t) \right) \left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right|\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_a^b \left( \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \bullet \frac{\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)}{\left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right|} \right) \left| \left| \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) \right| \right|\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_a^b \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \bullet \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)\,\mathrm{d} t\end{align*}$$

In fact, this suggests an even more compact and descriptive way to write a path integral through a vector field: just like we write $\mathrm{d} u = u'(x)\,\mathrm{d} x$ when doing $u$-substitution, we can write $\mathrm{d}\vec{r} = \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)\,\mathrm{d} t$, making the path integral representable (again, pre-parameterization) as

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \vec{F} \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{r}.\end{align*}$$

That was a lot of theory and symbol-pushing, so let’s pause and summarize what we’ve discovered so far before applying it to our running example with the circular path through the river. As is often the case, we’ve motivated everything in $\mathbb{R}^2$, but it will work just as well in higher dimensions.

Definition: path integral through a vector fields

Let $\vec{F}$ be a vector field and let $C$ be a path. The path integral of $\mathbf{\vec{F}}$ along $\mathbf{C}$ is the path integral along $C$ of the component of $\vec{F}$ parallel to $C$, and is written in three different ways:

$$\begin{align*}1.\ \int_C \vec{F} \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s\end{align*}$$

expresses it as an integral with respect to arc length of the scalar projection of $\vec{F}$ onto the unit tangent vector $\vec{T}$ of $C$, without needing to specify a parameterization $\vec{r}(t)$. It’s the farthest from an actual formula to compute the integral, but it displays most clearly what’s actually being integrated.

$$\begin{align*}2.\ \int_C \vec{F} \bullet \mathrm{d} \vec{r}\end{align*}$$

expresses the same concept, but indicates that with the choice of a parameterization $\vec{r}$ of $C$, the expression $\vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s$ can be simplified. It’s more theoretically opaque, since $\mathrm{d}\vec{r}$ isn’t as intuitive of an expression, but it reminds us how to actually compute these path integrals; namely,

$$\begin{align*}3.\ \int_a^b \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \bullet \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)\,\mathrm{d} t\end{align*}$$

presents an integral that we can actually compute. It’s noisier than the previous form, but it’s where we’ll start when we’re attempting to compute one of these path integrals.

With all that written out, let’s now return to our swimming example. As a reminder, $\vec{F}(x, y) = \left< -y, -x + y \right>$, and we’re swimming along a circular arc. That arc has radius $2$ and travels clockwise, so a good start for the parameterization is

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(t) &= \left< 2\cos(-t), 2\sin(-t) \right>\\[4px]\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) &= \left< 2\sin(-t), -2\cos(-t) \right>.\end{align*}$$

The starting point of $\left( 1, -\sqrt{3} \right)$ has an angle of $\arctan\left( \frac{-\sqrt{3}}{1} \right) = -\frac{\pi}{3}$, so we’ll start our parameterization at $t = \frac{\pi}{3}$ (since our formula already includes a minus sign). Similarly, we’ll end at $t = \frac{5\pi}{3}$. Now our integral becomes

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \vec{F} \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{r} &= \int_{\pi / 3}^{5 \pi / 3} \vec{F}(\vec{r}(t)) \bullet \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_{\pi / 3}^{5 \pi / 3} \left< -2\sin(-t), -2\cos(-t) + 2\sin(-t) \right> \bullet \left< 2\sin(-t), -2\cos(-t) \right>\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_{\pi / 3}^{5 \pi / 3} \left( -4\sin^2(-t) + 4\cos^2(-t) - 4\sin(-t)\cos(-t) \right)\,\mathrm{d} t.\end{align*}$$

Be careful not to write $-\sin^2(-t) + \cos^2(-t) = 1$! That’s only true when the sine and cosine terms have the same sign. Instead, we can use a trig identity that equates that with $\cos(-2t)$, and a similar one for the remaining term in the integral.

$$\begin{align*}&= \int_{\pi / 3}^{5 \pi / 3} \left( 4\cos(-2t) - 2\sin(-2t) \right)\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \left. \left[ -2\sin(-2t) - \cos(-2t) \right] \right|_{\pi / 3}^{5 \pi / 3}\\[4px]&= \left( -2\sin\left( -\frac{10 \pi}{3} \right) - \cos\left( -\frac{10 \pi}{3} \right) \right) - \left( -2\sin\left( -\frac{2 \pi}{3} \right) - \cos\left( -\frac{2 \pi}{3} \right) \right)\\[4px]&= \left( -2\left( \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \right) - \left( -\frac{1}{2} \right) \right) - \left( -2\left( -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \right) - \left( -\frac{1}{2} \right) \right)\\[4px]&= -2\sqrt{3}\end{align*}$$

That’s the same result we got from the straight-line path! Rather unsurprisingly, we’ll soon be able to explain why this was no coincidence, but the very idea might well raise some red flags. For all their fancy presentation, path integrals through vector fields are just regular path integrals of a particular dot product, and we just discussed how path integrals aren’t path-dependent. The only thing that’s specific to a path integral through a vector field is the function we integrate, not the path we take, and so if we’re going to conclude that some of these integrals are path-independent, it will have to be a property of the integrand. We’ll get there momentarily, but we’re in desperate need of practice right now.

Exercise: path integrals through a vector field

Let $\displaystyle \vec{F} = \left< x^2, x - 2y \right>$.

1. Let $L$ be the straight-line path from $(-1, -1)$ to $(2, 2)$. Find $\displaystyle \int_L \vec{F} \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s$.

2. Let $P$ be the path from $(-1, -1)$ to $(2, 2)$ that travels along the parabola $y = x^2 - 2$, as shown. Do you expect Find $\displaystyle \int_P \vec{F} \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s$ to be the same as your answer to question 1?

3. Evaluate the integral in question 2 and find out.

Let’s work through both of them and find out! We can parameterize the straight-line path $L$ as

$$\begin{align*}\vec{l}(t) &= \left< -1 + 3t, -1 + 3t \right>, \quad 0 \leq t \leq 1\\[4px]\vec{l}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) &= \left< 3, 3 \right>,\end{align*}$$

so the path integral is

$$\begin{align*}\int_L \vec{F} \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s &= \int_L \vec{F} \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{l}\\[4px]&= \int_0^1 \vec{F}(\vec{l}(t)) \bullet \vec{l}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_0^1 \left< \left( -1 + 3t \right)^2, -1 + 3t - 2\left( -1 + 3t \right) \right> \bullet \left< 3, 3 \right>\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_0^1 \left< 1 - 6t + 9t^2, 1 - 3t \right> \bullet \left< 3, 3 \right>\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_0^1 \left( 6 - 27t + 27t^2 \right)\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \left. \left[ 6t - \frac{27}{2}t^2 + 9t^3 \right] \right|_0^1\\[4px]&= \frac{3}{2}.\end{align*}$$

There are many ways to parameterize this parabola, but one of the simplest is

$$\begin{align*}\vec{p}(t) &= \left< t, t^2 - 2 \right>, \quad -1 \leq t \leq 2\\[4px]\vec{p}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) &= \left< 1, 2t \right>,\end{align*}$$

making the path integral

$$\begin{align*}\int_L \vec{F} \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{p} &= \int_{-1}^2 \vec{F}(\vec{p}(t)) \bullet \vec{p}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_{-1}^2 \left< t^2, t - 2\left( t^2 - 2 \right) \right> \bullet \left< 1, 2t \right>\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_{-1}^2 \left< t^2, t - 2t^2 + 4 \right> \bullet \left< 1, 2t \right>\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_{-1}^2 \left( t^2 + 2t^2 - 4t^3 + 8t \right)\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_{-1}^2 \left( 3t^2 - 4t^3 + 8t \right)\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \left. \left[ t^3 - t^4 + 4t^2 \right] \right|_{-1}^2\\[4px]&= 6.\end{align*}$$

Not the same result! This seems fairly reasonable based on the graph, too: along $L$, the field is either barely present at all or slightly helping our motion (the faint green tint), whereas $P$ experiences a moderate amount of hindrance (the purple at the start and faintly at the end), combined with a large stretch of help in the middle.

The Fundamental Theorem of Path Integrals

Let’s properly dig into path-independence. Why do some path integrals, but not all, seem to depend only on their endpoints, rather than the path between them? In single-variable calculus, the fundamental theorem of calculus resulted in something similar to path independence by expressing the entire integral $\int_a^b f(x)\,\mathrm{d} x$ in terms of an antiderivative $F$ of $f$ at the endpoints of $[a, b]$; if we could do the same thing for a certain kind of path integral, we’d have not only path independence, but a formula that we could use to skip their computation entirely.

The only thing in the fundamental theorem of calculus that we haven’t considered for path integrals is the presence of an antiderivative. An equivalent way to phrase the single-variable FTC is

$$\begin{align*}\int_a^b f'(x)\,\mathrm{d} x = f(b) - f(a)\end{align*}$$

for any differentiable function $f$ with a continuous derivative. This phrasing is normally just to get around needing to define $F$ separately, but it also points us in a particular direction: What if we consider only path integrals of derivatives? The issue is that there isn’t really a derivative-like operator for a function; the closest thing we have is the gradient, which outputs a vector field. But we just spent the last subsection talking about how to take path integrals through vector fields! Let’s see what we can do.

Given a differentiable function $f(x, y)$ so that $\nabla\! f$ is continuous, let’s consider

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \nabla\! f \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s &= \int_C \nabla\! f \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{r},\end{align*}$$

where $C$ is a path parameterized by $\vec{r}(t)$ for $a \leq t \leq b$. This expands as usual to

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \nabla\! f \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{r} &= \int_a^b \nabla\! f\left( \vec{r}(t) \right) \bullet \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t)\,\mathrm{d} t.\end{align*}$$

We’re getting pretty symbol-dense, and we need to expand that gradient, which threatens to make things worse, so let’s pause and focus only on the inside of the integral. Let’s also write $\vec{r}(t) = \left< x(t), y(t) \right>$ to make those functions more explicit. Now the expression in the integral is

$$\begin{align*}\nabla\! f\left( \vec{r}(t) \right) \bullet \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) &= \nabla\! f( x(t), y(t) ) \bullet \left< x'(t), y'(t) \right>\\[4px]&= \left< f_x( x(t), y(t) ), \ f_y( x(t), y(t) ) \right>\ \bullet\ \left< x'(t), y'(t) \right>\\[4px]&= f_x( x(t), y(t) )\, x'(t) + f_y( x(t), y(t) )\, y'(t)\\[4px]&= \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \frac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}t}.\end{align*}$$

Back once again, it’s our friend the multivariable chain rule! This is the result of differentiating a composition of two functions: here, it says that

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} \left[ f\left( \vec{r}(t) \right) \right] = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{\mathrm{d}t} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \frac{\mathrm{d}y}{\mathrm{d}t},\end{align*}$$

which means

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} \left[ f\left( \vec{r}(t) \right) \right] = \nabla\! f\left( \vec{r}(t) \right) \bullet \vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t).\end{align*}$$

As a remark, even if this didn’t help us with our current problem (and it absolutely does), this is a lovely presentation of the multivariable chain rule. The single-variable chain rule

$$\begin{align*}\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} \left[ f(r(t)) \right] = f'(r(t)) \cdot r'(t)\end{align*}$$

has gracefully adapted to higher dimensions by substituting a gradient for the outer derivative and a dot product for the multiplication, and it’s elegance like this that leads many mathematicians to declare that their subject is discovered, not invented.

We currently need that elegance as means to a very utilitarian end, though. The whole expression we were considering lived inside an integral, and putting it back, we find

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \nabla\! f \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{r} &= \int_a^b \frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}t} \left[ f\left( \vec{r}(t) \right) \right]\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \left. \left[ f\left( \vec{r}(t) \right) \right] \right|_a^b\\[4px]&= f\left( \vec{r}(b) \right) - f\left( \vec{r}(a) \right).\end{align*}$$

But $\vec{r}(a)$ and $\vec{r}(b)$ are the endpoints of $C$, and so we have just the result we were hoping for! Let’s collect our results together with a proper theorem statement.

The Fundamental Theorem of Path Integrals

Let $\vec{F}$ be a continuous conservative vector field — that is, $\vec{F} = \nabla\! f$ for some function $f$ — and let $C$ be a path from the point $p$ to the point $q$. Then

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \vec{F} \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s &= f(q) - f(p).\end{align*}$$

This is more commonly called the Fundamental Theorem of Line Integrals or the Gradient Theorem, but we’ll stick with our more descriptive nomenclature.

We can immediately get so much from this theorem! First of all, we finally have the result that path integrals of conservative vector fields are path-independent, since they only depend on the values of the potential function (the function $f$ in the theorem statement) at the endpoints of the path. Also, if we integrate a conservative vector field along any closed loop (a path that ends where it begins), we get zero, since $p = q$. Path integrals along closed loops come up quite a bit, especially in physics, and occasionally are indicated with a circle drawn in the integral symbol, as in

$$\begin{align*}\oint_C f(x, y) \,\mathrm{d} s.\end{align*}$$

Just like the use of the $\partial$ symbol to indicate a partial derivative instead of $\mathrm{d}$, the $\oint$ symbol means exactly the same thing as $\int$, and only looks a little different to help remind us what we’re doing. I’ll use the $\oint$ symbol in an effort to provide a little bit more context, but it’s entirely optional.

We can concisely write our latest result as

$$\begin{align*}\oint_C \vec{F} \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{r} = 0\end{align*}$$
