Section 6.6: Stokes’ Theorem

While surface integrals were complicated — maybe even the most technically complicated object we’ll study in the course — the path to the summit is now largely clear. In this section, we’ll examine the result of bringing the circulation form of Green’s Theorem to $\mathbb{R}^3$; to begin, let’s take a region and its boundary that live in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and transplant them as delicately as possible into 3D space.

Let $D$ be the unit disk in the $xy$-plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$; that is,

$$\begin{align*}D = \left\{ (x, y, 0) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \mid x^2 + y^2 \leq 1 \right\}.\end{align*}$$

As an object in $\mathbb{R}^2$, Green’s theorem tells that for any 2D field $\left< f, g \right>$,

$$\begin{align*}\int_{\partial D} \left< f, g \right> \bullet \mathrm{d} \vec{r} = \iint_D \operatorname{curl} \left< f, g \right> \,\mathrm{d} A = \iint_D \operatorname{curl} \left( g_x - f_y \right) \,\mathrm{d} A\end{align*}$$

where $\partial D$ is the unit circle, oriented counterclockwise.

Let’s now begin to translate each of the elements of that Green’s Theorem equation into $\mathbb{R}^3$. The first and most notable change is that $D$ is now a surface, and so the right-hand side of Green’s Theorem has to become a surface integral. Next, the vector field may have a $z$-component, so let’s let $\vec{F} = \left< f, g, h \right>$ be a general field in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Then $\operatorname{curl}\vec{F} = \nabla\!\times\! \vec{F}$ is a vector instead of a scalar, and so that new surface integral actually needs to be of a vector field:

$$\begin{align*}\iint_D \left( \nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} \right) \bullet \vec{n} \,\mathrm{d}\sigma.\end{align*}$$

What is that unit normal vector $\vec{n}$ going to be? Since $D$ is perfectly flat, it should be either $\vec{k}$ or $-\vec{k}$, and we can see which one is correct by expanding the curl. We have

$$\begin{align*}\nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} = \left< h_y - g_z,\ f_z - h_x,\ g_x - f_y \right>,\end{align*}$$

so to keep the function inside the surface integral equal to $g_x - f_y$ as it was in Green’s Theorem, we should take $\vec{n} = \vec{k}$, orienting the surface so that the normal vectors point upward.

The last object to consider is the curve integral on the left side. While the integral itself translates just fine to $\mathbb{R}^3$ — we’ve been taking curve integrals in 3D space ever since we introduced them — the orientation of $\partial D$ takes a little more care. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, we said it had to be positively-oriented, which meant that if we walked around it, we’d keep $D$ on our left. For this circle, that meant a counterclockwise orientation, but that’s a somewhat more delicate notion in $\mathbb{R}^3$: turn that previous graph upside-down, and the counterclockwise orientation will seemingly become clockwise! To avoid this, we’ll need to add a single caveat to oriented boundaries of surfaces so that we avoid this ambiguity.

Definition: oriented boundary of a surface

Let $S$ be a smooth, oriented surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The positively-oriented boundary of $S$ is the closed curve $\partial S$, oriented so that walking along $\partial S$ while staying parallel to the normal vector for $S$ keeps $S$ on the left.

For example, this cap of a sphere oriented so the convex side points outward has a boundary oriented as shown. If we reversed the orientation, so that the red vectors pointed inward instead, the orientation of the blue boundary would be reversed.

The mathematical way to phrase this definition is that an orientation of the surface induces an orientation of the boundary. There’s also a connection with the right-hand rule, in that if you curl the fingers in the direction of the boundary, then your thumb should roughly point in the direction of the normal vectors to the surface.

Let $C$ be the cylinder of radius $1$ and height $2$ centered at the origin and parallel to the $z$-axis, oriented so that the top boundary circle has a counterclockwise orientation when viewed from above. Determine whether the orientation on $C$ is outward or inward and find the orientation of the other boundary circle.

2. (Challenge question) Let $S$ be the surface given by the graph of $z = \cos(x) + \cos(y) + 2$ lying above the circle of radius $\pi$ centered at the origin, oriented so that flux of the field $\vec{F} = \left< 0, 0, z \right>$ through $S$ is negative. Find a parameterization for $S$, and then find a parameterization for the oriented boundary $\partial S$, making sure the boundary parameterization is oriented correctly.

1. Walking around the inside of the top circle in its oriented direction keeps the surface on our left, so the cylinder needs to be oriented inward.

On the bottom, walking around the inside of the cylinder only keeps the surface on our left if we walk clockwise when viewed from above — that means the two boundary circles necessarily have different orientations!

2. We can try to parameterize $S$ as

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v}) = \left< u,\ \mathrm{v},\ \cos(u) + \cos(\mathrm{v}) + 2 \right>,\end{align*}$$

which would result in

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}_u &= \left< 1, 0, -\sin(u) \right>\\[4px]\vec{r}_{\mathrm{v}} &= \left< 0, 1, -\sin(\mathrm{v}) \right>\\[4px]\vec{r}_u \times \vec{r}_{\mathrm{v}} &= \left< \cos(u),\ \cos(\mathrm{v}),\ 1 \right>.\end{align*}$$

However, that would always have a nonnegative dot product with the field $\vec{F}$, since $S$ lives on or above the $xy$-plane. Therefore, we need to reverse the orientation of $S$, which we can do by swapping the roles of $u$ and $\mathrm{v}$:

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v}) &= \left< \mathrm{v},\ u,\ \cos(\mathrm{v}) + \cos(u) + 2 \right>\\[4px]\vec{r}_u \times \vec{r}_{\mathrm{v}} &= \left< -\sin(\mathrm{v}),\, -\sin(u),\, -1 \right>,\end{align*}$$

for $(u, \mathrm{v}) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ with $u^2 + \mathrm{v}^2 \leq \pi^2$. Now the boundary of $S$ is given by the boundary of the $u\mathrm{v}$-domain: that is, $u^2 + \mathrm{v}^2 = \pi^2$. If the normal vectors to the surface point mostly downward, then we want to walk around the circle with our head roughly pointing downward. To keep $S$ on our left, we need to walk counterclockwise from the perspective of looking up at the circle from below, which is clockwise when viewed from above.

Ordinarily, we would want to parameterize the boundary in the $u\mathrm{v}$-plane with

$$\begin{align*}u &= \pi\cos(-t)\\[4px]\mathrm{v} &= \pi\sin(-t)\end{align*}$$

for $t \in [0, 2\pi]$, with the $-t$ making the circle clockwise. However, since $\mathrm{v}$ is representing the $x$-coordinate and $u$ is representing the $y$-coordinate in $\vec{r}$, we’ll want to swap the formulas for $u$ and $\mathrm{v}$:

$$\begin{align*}\mathrm{v} &= \pi\cos(-t)\\[4px]u &= \pi\sin(-t)\end{align*}$$

Then the total parameterization comes from plugging this into $\vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v})$ to map it onto the surface: $\partial S$ is parameterized by

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v}) &= \left< \mathrm{v},\ u,\ \cos(\mathrm{v}) + \cos(u) + 2 \right>\\[4px]&= \left< \pi\cos(-t),\ \pi\sin(-t),\ \cos(\pi\cos(-t)) + \cos(\pi\sin(-t)) + 2 \right>.\end{align*}$$

With all of the pieces translated to 3D, we’re ready to state the theorem. Happily, there are no extra surprises!

Stokes’ Theorem

Let $S$ be a smooth, oriented surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ whose positively-oriented boundary $\partial S$ is a path (i.e. a piecewise-smooth closed curve), and let $\vec{F}$ be a vector field whose components have continuous partial derivatives on and around $S$. Then

$$\begin{align*}\int_{\partial S} \vec{F} \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{r} = \iint_S \left( \nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} \right) \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{\sigma}.\end{align*}$$

In other words, the circulation around the boundary of $S$ is equal to the integral of the curl across $S$.

Stokes’ Theorem is intuitively true for the same reason Green’s Theorem is: dotting the curl vector $\nabla\!\times\! \vec{F}$ with the surface’s normal vector $\vec{n}$ gives the infinitesimal circulation of $\vec{F}$ in the surface at each point, and when we integrate those infinitesimal circulations, interior ones cancel out, leaving only the circulation on the boundary. If there’s one new thing we can take from Stokes’ theorem, it’s that we made a good choice with the generalization of curl to $\mathbb{R}^3$; it may be unusual that it became a vector while divergence remained a scalar, but this theorem provides strong evidence that that was correct.

Example: Stokes’ Theorem

Let $\vec{F}$ be the vector field

$$\begin{align*}\vec{F}(x, y, z) = \left< -y^3, x^3, z^3 \right>.\end{align*}$$

Find the circulation of $\vec{F}$ around the circle $C$ of radius $2$ parallel to the $xy$-plane centered at $(0, 0, 3)$, oriented counterclockwise when looking at it from above.

To do this directly, we would need to parameterize $C$; let’s take

$$\begin{align*}\vec{s}(t) = \left< 2\cos(t),\ 2\sin(t),\ 3 \right>\end{align*}$$

for $t \in [0, 2\pi]$, which results in circulation integral of

$$\begin{align*}\int_{C} \vec{F} \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{s} &= \int_0^{2\pi} \left< -8\sin^3(t),\ 8\cos^3(t),\ 27 \right> \bullet \left< -2\sin(t),\ 2\cos(t),\ 0 \right>\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( 16\sin^4(t) + 16\cos^4(t) \right)\mathrm{d} t,\end{align*}$$

and while it’s possible to evaluate that integral, it’s a lot of work that we could avoid; let’s try Stokes’ Theorem instead. When we used Green’s Theorem to translate curve integrals into double integrals, there was only one choice of region whose oriented boundary was the curve we were given. In $\mathbb{R}^3$, though, there are infinitely many surfaces whose boundary is any given curve! The simplest possible one we could take here is the filled-in disk, oriented upward — toggle it on in the Desmos graph to see. However, we could just as well take the surface given by $z = \frac{3}{4}\left( x^2 + y^2 \right)$ for $z \leq 3$ or $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 13$ for $z \geq 3$, both oriented upward. Toggle those on to compare; if you’re wondering where those constants came from, remember that we want $x^2 + y^2 = 4$ and $z = 3$ to satisfy whatever equation we choose.

So which surface should we pick? When in doubt, pick the flattest surface possible. It’s easier to parameterize and has a simpler normal vector, so unless there’s something special about the curl of the field that makes it particularly nice on a portion of a sphere or something similar, flat surfaces are our best bet. In our case, the curl of $\vec{F}$ is

$$\begin{align*}\nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} = \left< 0, 0, 3x^2 + 3y^2 \right>,\end{align*}$$

which looks well-suited to the flat disk if $x$ and $y$ are parameterized with $\cos$ and $\sin$. Specifically, we can parameterize that disk by functionally using polar coordinates as

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v}) = \left< u\cos(\mathrm{v}),\ u\sin(\mathrm{v}),\ 3 \right>\end{align*}$$

for $u \in [0, 2]$ and $\mathrm{v} \in [0, 2\pi]$. Then

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}_u &= \left< \cos(\mathrm{v}),\ \sin(\mathrm{v}),\ 0 \right>\\[4px]\vec{r}_{\mathrm{v}} &= \left< -u\sin(\mathrm{v}),\ u\cos(\mathrm{v}),\ 0 \right>\\[4px]\vec{r}_u \times \vec{r}_{\mathrm{v}} &= \left< 0, 0, u\sin^2(\mathrm{v}) + u^2\cos^2(\mathrm{v}) \right>\\[4px]&= \left< 0, 0, u \right>,\end{align*}$$

which is correctly oriented upward, since $u \geq 0$. As a side note, the magnitude of the cross product equaling $u$ here is no coincidence: $u$ is the variable we usually call $r$ in polar coordinates, and so one way to think about a polar double integral is as a reparameterization of a scalar surface integral over a flat surface, where the extra factor of $u = r$ comes exactly from $\left| \left| \vec{r}_u \times \vec{r}_{\mathrm{v}} \right| \right|$.

Back to our problem. We’re interested in a vector surface integral, not a scalar one, so we just need the cross product and not its magnitude. If we call the disk $D$, then $\partial D = C$, so

$$\begin{align*}\int_C \vec{F} \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{s} &= \iint_D \left( \nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} \right) \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{\sigma}\\[4px]&= \int_0^{2\pi} \!\! \int_0^2 \left< 0, 0, 3\left( u\cos(\mathrm{v}) \right)^2 + 3\left( u\sin(\mathrm{v}) \right)^2 \right> \bullet \left< 0, 0, u \right>\mathrm{d} u\,\mathrm{d}\mathrm{v}\\[4px]&= \int_0^{2\pi} \!\! \int_0^2 \left( 3u^3\cos^2(\mathrm{v}) + 3u^3\sin^2(\mathrm{v}) \right)\mathrm{d} u\,\mathrm{d}\mathrm{v}\\[4px]&= \int_0^{2\pi} \!\! \int_0^2 3u^3\,\mathrm{d} u\,\mathrm{d}\mathrm{v}\\[4px]&= 24\pi.\end{align*}$$

We’ll return to the idea of being able to choose different surfaces to represent the same integral soon, but let’s first get some practice with Stokes’ Theorem itself.

Exercise: Stokes’ Theorem

1. Let $\vec{F}$ be a field defined by

$$\begin{align*}\vec{F}(x, y, z) = \left< x + 2z,\ 2y - 3x,\ 5y + z \right>.\end{align*}$$

Find the circulation of $\vec{F}$ around the boundary of the portion of the plane $P$ given by $x + y + z = 2$ in the first octant, oriented counterclockwise when looking at it from the origin.

2. Let $\vec{G}(x, y, z) = \left< z^2 - y,\, x+z,\, xy \right>$, and let $S$ be the top half of the unit sphere, oriented downward. Compute

$$\begin{align*}\iint_S \left( \nabla\!\times\! \vec{G} \right) \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{\sigma}.\end{align*}$$

1. Computing that circulation directly is certainly possible, but it would be annoying: each edge would need to be parameterized separately. Instead, let’s see if the curl is any nicer. It comes out to

$$\begin{align*}\nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} = \left< 5, 2, -3 \right>,\end{align*}$$

which looks dramatically simpler! We can parameterize the surface by

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(u, \mathrm{v}) = \left< u,\, \mathrm{v},\, 2 - u - \mathrm{v} \right>\end{align*}$$

for $(u, \mathrm{v})$ in the triangle $T$ in the $u\mathrm{v}$-plane with vertices at $(0, 0)$, $(2, 0)$, and $(0, 2)$. Checking our orientation,

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}_u = \left< 1, 0, -1 \right>\\[4px]\vec{r}_{\mathrm{v}} = \left< 0, 1, -1 \right>\\[4px]\vec{r}_u \times \vec{r}_{\mathrm{v}} = \left< 1, 1, 1 \right>,\end{align*}$$

which points away from the origin. That would make the orientation of the boundary counterclockwise when viewed from the perspective shown above, which isn’t right. Rather than reworking our entire parameterization, though, let’s just negate the value of the cross product and therefore the surface integral:

$$\begin{align*}\int_{\partial P} \vec{F} \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s &= \iint_P \left( \nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} \right) \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{\sigma}\\[4px]&= \iint_T \left< 5, 2, -3 \right> \bullet \left( -\vec{r}_u \times \vec{r}_{\mathrm{v}} \right)\mathrm{d} A\\[4px]&= \iint_T \left< 5, 2, -3 \right> \bullet \left< -1, -1, -1 \right>\mathrm{d} A\\[4px]&= \iint_T -4\,\mathrm{d} A\\[4px]&= -4 \left| T \right|\\[4px]&= -4(2)\\[4px]&= -8.\end{align*}$$

2. This one is set up directly for use with Stokes’ Theorem, which is rather common for problems like this: it’s possible, but tedious and difficult, to reverse-engineer a vector field given its curl. Instead, we’re often told that we’re integrating a curl and given the original field directly.

Here, the curl evaluates to $\nabla\!\times\! \vec{G} = \left< x - 1,\, 2z - y,\, 2 \right>$, and while we could integrate that over the surface, it’s nice to avoid unnecessary surface parameterizations and integrals if at all possible, particularly when spheres are involved. Instead, let’s look at the boundary of $S$: it’s where the hemisphere meets the plane $z = 0$, so it’s the unit circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ in the $xy$-plane. Since $S$ is oriented downward, we need to walk around the circle clockwise when viewed from above to keep it on our left.

Now we can use Stokes’ Theorem to switch from integrating the curl over the hemisphere to integrating the field itself around the boundary: with

$$\begin{align*}\vec{r}(t) &= \left< \cos(-t),\ \sin(-t),\ 0 \right>\\[4px]\vec{r}\hspace{0.1em}'(t) &= \left< \sin(-t),\, -\cos(-t),\ 0 \right>\end{align*}$$

parameterizing the unit circle clockwise, we have

$$\begin{align*}\iint_S \left( \nabla\!\times\! \vec{G} \right) \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{\sigma} &= \int_{\partial S} \vec{G} \bullet \mathrm{d}\vec{r}\\[4px]&= \int_0^{2\pi} \left< -\sin(-t),\ \cos(-t),\ \cos(-t)\sin(-t) \right> \bullet \left< \sin(-t),\, -\cos(-t),\ 0 \right>\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_0^{2\pi} \left( -\sin^2(-t) - \cos^2(-t) \right)\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= \int_0^{2\pi} -1\,\mathrm{d} t\\[4px]&= -2\pi.\end{align*}$$

Incompressible Fields and Surface-Independence

In many ways, Stokes’ Theorem is the most direct higher-dimensional analogue of the Fundamental Theorem of Curve Integrals. That let us express a circulation integral of a field $\vec{F}$ in terms of a potential function $f$ on the boundary of the curve, but only if such a function $f$ with $\vec{F} = \nabla\! f$ existed. That was the case if $\vec{F}$ was conservative, meaning $\nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} = \vec{0}$, and for such fields, the theorem showed that their circulation was path-independent, depending only on the endpoints of any path taken.

Now, Stokes’ Theorem lets us express the flux of a field $\vec{F}$ through a surface in terms of the circulation of a different field $\vec{G}$ on the boundary, but only if such a field $\vec{G}$ with $\nabla\!\times\! \vec{G} = \vec{F}$ exists. To keep the analogy going, let’s define terms equivalent to potential function, conservativity, and path-independence for this new situation.

Definition: vector potential

Let $\vec{F}$ be a vector field defined on all of $\mathbb{R}^3$. A vector potential for $\vec{F}$ is a field $\vec{G}$ with $\nabla\!\times\! \vec{G} = \vec{F}$.

When we began discussing vector fields with potential functions, we noticed they were conservative, since $\nabla\!\times\! \nabla\! f = \vec{0}$ for reasonably well-behaved functions $f$ by Clairaut’s theorem. Wonderfully, the reverse was true, and for any reasonably well-behaved field $\vec{F}$, knowing it’s conservative is enough to guarantee it has a potential function. This time around, there’s a very similar story: for any field $\vec{F}$ whose entries all have continuous first partial derivatives, $\nabla\!\bullet\! (\nabla\!\times\! f) = 0$, again by Clairaut’s theorem, and it’s just as wonderful that the converse is true.

Theorem: divergence-zero fields have vector potentials

Let $\vec{F}$ be a vector field defined on all of $\mathbb{R}^3$. If $\nabla\!\bullet\! \vec{F} = 0$, then $\vec{F}$ has a vector potential.

This property will serve as our equivalent to conservativity, so let’s give it a name. Conservative came from conservation of energy — the stereotypical conservative field is gravity — but the standard name for a divergence-zero field stems from interpreting it as a fluid. If the divergence is zero, then the density of the fluid is constant everywhere: there’s just as much flowing into any small region per second as flowing out of it.

Definition: incompressible field

A vector field $\vec{F}$ is incompressible if $\operatorname{div} \vec{F} = 0$ everywhere.

We’re using $\operatorname{div} \vec{F}$ rather than $\nabla\!\bullet\! \vec{F}$ since this definition works just as well for 2D fields, but we’ll only be using it for 3D ones from here on out. The results we’ve stated so far tell us that incompressible fields have vector potentials, and by Stokes’ Theorem, that means their flux through any surface depends only on their circulation along its boundary.
