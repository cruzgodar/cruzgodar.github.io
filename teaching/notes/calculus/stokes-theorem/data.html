Section 6.6: Stokes’ Theorem

While surface integrals were complicated — maybe even the most technically complicated object we’ll study in the course — the path to the summit is now largely clear. In this section, we’ll examine the result of bringing the circulation form of Green’s Theorem to $\mathbb{R}^3$; recall that in $\mathbb{R}^2$, Green’s theorem stated that for a region $R$ with positively-oriented boundary $\partial R$,

$$\begin{align*}\int_{\partial R} \vec{F} \bullet \vec{T}\,\mathrm{d} s = \iint_R \operatorname{curl} \vec{F} \,\mathrm{d} A.\end{align*}$$

Let’s analyze what differences we’d expect to see. The most noticeable change is that the flat region $R$ will be replaced by a surface $S$, since that’s the general object whose boundary $\partial S$ is a curve. That means the right-hand side will become a surface integral; in $\mathbb{R}^2$, $\operatorname{curl} \vec{F}$ was a scalar, but it’s now a vector, and so we’d expect the surface integral to be of a vector field. There’s also an unknown: we required the boundary $\partial R$ to be positively-oriented in $\mathbb{R}^2$, but what does a positive orientation mean in $\mathbb{R}^3$? If $S$ is the unit disk $x^2 + y^2 \leq 1$, then we can’t just say that the boundary $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ should be oriented counterclockwise: that would change depending on whether we’re looking at the circle from above or below.
