Section 6.6: Stokes’ Theorem

While surface integrals were complicated — maybe even the most technically complicated object we’ll study in the course — the path to the summit is now largely clear. In this section, we’ll examine the result of bringing the circulation form of Green’s Theorem to $\mathbb{R}^3$; to begin, let’s take a region and its boundary that live in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and transplant them as delicately as possible into 3D space.

Let $D$ be the unit disk in the $xy$-plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$; that is,

$$\begin{align*}D = \left\{ (x, y, 0) \in \mathbb{R}^3 \mid x^2 + y^2 \leq 1 \right\}.\end{align*}$$

As an object in $\mathbb{R}^2$, Green’s theorem tells that for any 2D field $\left< f, g \right>$,

$$\begin{align*}\int_{\partial D} \left< f, g \right> \bullet \mathrm{d} \vec{r} = \iint_D \operatorname{curl} \left< f, g \right> \,\mathrm{d} A = \iint_D \operatorname{curl} \left( g_x - f_y \right) \,\mathrm{d} A\end{align*}$$

where $\partial D$ is the unit circle, oriented counterclockwise.

Let’s now begin to translate each of the elements of that Green’s Theorem equation into $\mathbb{R}^3$. The first and most notable change is that $D$ is now a surface, and so the right-hand side of Green’s Theorem has to become a surface integral. Next, the vector field may have a $z$-component, so let’s let $\vec{F} = \left< f, g, h \right>$ be a general field in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Then $\operatorname{curl}\vec{F} = \nabla\!\times\! \vec{F}$ is a vector instead of a scalar, and so that new surface integral actually needs to be of a vector field:

$$\begin{align*}\iint_D \left( \nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} \right) \bullet \vec{n} \,\mathrm{d} s.\end{align*}$$

What is that unit normal vector $\vec{n}$ going to be? Since $D$ is perfectly flat, it should be either $\vec{k}$ or $-\vec{k}$, and we can see which one is correct by expanding the curl. We have

$$\begin{align*}\nabla\!\times\! \vec{F} = \left< h_y - g_z,\ f_z - h_x,\ g_x - f_y \right>,\end{align*}$$

so to keep the function inside the surface integral equal to $g_x - f_y$ as it was in Green’s Theorem, we should take $\vec{n} = \vec{k}$, orienting the surface so that the normal vectors point upward.

The last object to consider is the curve integral on the left side. While the integral itself translates just fine to $\mathbb{R}^3$ — we’ve been taking curve integrals in 3D space ever since we introduced them — the orientation of $\partial D$ takes a little more care. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, we said it had to be positively-oriented, which meant that if we walked around it, we’d keep $D$ on our left. For this circle, that meant a counterclockwise orientation, but that’s a somewhat more delicate notion in $\mathbb{R}^3$: turn that previous graph upside-down, and the counterclockwise orientation will seemingly become clockwise! To avoid this, we’ll need to add a single caveat to oriented boundaries of surfaces so that we avoid this ambiguity.

Definition: oriented boundary of a surface

Let $S$ be a smooth, oriented surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The positively-oriented boundary of $S$ is the curve $\partial S$, oriented so that walking along $\partial S$ while staying parallel to the normal vector for $S$ keeps $S$ on the left.
