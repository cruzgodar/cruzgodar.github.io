In problems 1–6, determine if the linear transformation $T$ is one-to-one, if it is onto, and if it is invertible. If it is invertible, find the inverse transformation.

1. $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ defined by $T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} x \\ y \end{array}\right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 2x + y \\ x + 2y\end{array}\right]$.

2. $T: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ defined by $T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} x \\ y \\ z \end{array}\right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} x \\ y + z \end{array}\right]$.

3. $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$, where

$$\begin{align*}T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 0 \end{array}\right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 4 \end{array}\right] \qquad T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 4 \\ 6 \end{array}\right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 4 \\ -10 \end{array}\right].\end{align*}$$

4. $T: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$, where

$$\begin{align*}T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 3 \\ 7 \\ 4 \end{array}\right] \qquad T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 2 \\ 0 \end{array}\right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{array}\right] \qquad T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{array}\right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 6 \\ 16 \\ 7 \end{array}\right].\end{align*}$$

5. $T: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$, where

$$\begin{align*}T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ -1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]\right) = -3 \qquad T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]\right) = 1 \qquad T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]\right) = 4.\end{align*}$$

6. $T: \mathbb{R}^5 \to \mathbb{R}^2$, where

$$\begin{align*}T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \\ 2 \\ 3 \\ 0 \end{array}\right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \end{array}\right] \qquad T\left(\left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 1 \\ 2 \\ -1 \\ 3 \end{array}\right]\right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 2 \\ 0 \end{array}\right].\end{align*}$$

7. Let $R : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ be a function (not necessarily a linear transformation) defined by rotating its inputs $90^\circ$ counterclockwise. For example, $R\left( \left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 0 \end{array}\right] \right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 1 \end{array}\right]$ and $R\left( \left[\begin{array}{c} \frac{1}{2} \\ \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \end{array}\right] \right) = \left[\begin{array}{c} -\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \\ \frac{1}{2} \end{array}\right]$.

a) Explain with a picture why $R$ is in fact a linear transformation, i.e. $R(\vec{v_1} + \vec{v_2}) = R(\vec{v_1}) + R(\vec{v_2})$ and $R(c\vec{v}) = cR(\vec{v})$.

b) Find the matrix for $R$.

c) Let $R_\theta : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ be the linear transformation that rotates its inputs an angle $\theta$ counterclockwise, where $\theta$ is a variable. Find a matrix for $R_\theta$.

In problems 8–12, compute the determinant of the given matrix.

a) The identity matrix $I$ has the same determinant no matter what size it is. What is $\det I$?

b) Let $A$ be an invertible matrix. Using part a), find $\det A^{-1}$ in terms of $\det A$.
